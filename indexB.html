The Energy Crisis

May, 2024 by Jack

Origin

The origin of the Energy Crisis:

The energy crisis started in 2021 after the corona-virus pandemic. In February of 2022, the problem dramatically increased after Russia invaded and declared war against Ukraine. After Russia invaded Ukraine, they stopped the important delivery of gases from Russia that went to the entire world.

Development

How the Crisis has developed.

In 2021, the crisis was slow, but rapidly increased after Russia invaded Ukraine. Over the span of 2022, energy prices rose, and more households (up to 5 million) ended up in fuel and energy poverty. At the start of 2023, the energy and gas prices slowly went down again, and it has been slowly going down since then. Luckily, the prices continue to go down and more households can afford energy and gas again, but there are still 6 million households that live in fuel poverty (last update in April 2024).

Effect (P1)

The effect of the Energy Crisis

There is no direct effect on the world relating to the energy crisis. However, it had and still has a massive effect on humans. The energy crisis caused problems like slow economic growth, increase of inflation and unemployment. This was visible in 2022, when many companies suffered from the energy crisis and many people became unemployed.

Effect (P2)

The effect of the Energy Crisis on Regular People

Due to the energy crisis, not only big companies suffered but normal individuals did as well. Gas, oil and energy prices increased rapidly, and many people with lower incomes struggled. In April of 2024, over 6 million households in the United Kingdom still suffer from energy poverty. Even after the prices got lowered in 2022, many people have still not recovered from the damage.

Conclusion


In summary, the energy crisis that began in 2021 and escalated with Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 has had widespread impacts, including increased fuel poverty, economic stagnation, inflation, and unemployment. Although energy prices have gradually decreased, millions of households, especially in the UK, remain in energy poverty, illustrating the crisis's lingering effects. This situation underscores the need for resilient and sustainable energy policies to support those still affected and prevent future crises.
